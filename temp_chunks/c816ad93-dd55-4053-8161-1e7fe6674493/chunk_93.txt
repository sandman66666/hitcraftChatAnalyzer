**[TLDR]**: Music royalties are payments made to rights holders (like songwriters, artists, and producers) for the use of their music. They come in various forms, such as mechanical royalties, performance royalties, and sync licenses.

**[Main Response]**: 

1. **Mechanical Royalties**: These are paid to songwriters and music publishers whenever a song is reproduced physically (like on CDs or vinyl) or digitally (like downloads or streaming). In the U.S., for example, the current mechanical royalty rate is set at 9.1 cents per song per copy for physical copies and downloads.

2. **Performance Royalties**: These are earned when a song is performed publicly. This includes live performances, radio play, and plays on streaming services. Performance royalties are collected by Performance Rights Organizations (PROs) like ASCAP, BMI, or SESAC in the U.S., which distribute the royalties to the rights holders.

3. **Sync Licenses**: These are fees paid for the use of music in visual media, such as films, TV shows, or commercials. If a song is used in a movie, the producer typically needs to obtain a sync license, which compensates the songwriter and publisher.

4. **Print Royalties**: If your music is transcribed into sheet music, you can also earn print royalties from those sales.

### Tips for Adding Vocal Reverb

1. **Choose the Right Type of Reverb**: Different reverbs can create different atmospheres:
   - **Plate Reverb**: Smooth and bright, great for vocals.
   - **Room Reverb**: Gives a natural feel, as if the vocals are in a small space.
   - **Hall Reverb**: Adds a lush, expansive quality suitable for ballads.

2. **Adjust the Wet/Dry Mix**: Start with a low wet mix (around 10-20%) and adjust to taste. You want the reverb to enhance the vocal without overwhelming it.

3. **Use Pre-Delay**: Setting a pre-delay (a short time before the reverb starts) can help keep the vocals clear and intelligible. A value of around 20-50ms is common.

4. **Tailor the EQ**: If your reverb plugin has an EQ option, consider cutting some low frequencies (below 200Hz) to avoid muddiness and maybe boosting the highs for brightness.

5. **Automate Reverb**: If certain parts of the song need more space (like a chorus), consider automating the reverb amount for dynamic effect.
