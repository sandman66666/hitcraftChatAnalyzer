**[Main Response]**: 
A chord progression is a series of chords played in a sequence. The choice of chords and their arrangement can evoke different emotions and atmospheres. Here are a few common types of chord progressions:

1. **I-IV-V**: This is one of the most basic and frequently used progressions in popular music. For example, in the key of C major, the chords would be:
   - I (C) - IV (F) - V (G)

2. **I-vi-ii-V**: This progression provides a nice, flowing sound and is often used in jazz and pop music. In C major, it would be:
   - I (C) - vi (Am) - ii (Dm) - V (G)

3. **I-V-vi-iii-IV**: A popular progression in many pop songs. In C major, it would be:
   - I (C) - V (G) - vi (Am) - iii (Em) - IV (F)

4. **ii-V-I**: This is a classic jazz progression that resolves nicely. In C major, it would be:
   - ii (Dm) - V (G) - I (C)

**[Deep Dive]**: 
To expand on the concept, there are some important things to consider when working with chord progressions:

- **Function of Chords**: Each chord serves a functionâ€”tonic (I), subdominant (IV), and dominant (V). Understanding these roles will help you create tension and resolution in your music.
  
- **Modal Interchange**: You can borrow chords from parallel modes (like using a bVII from the C Mixolydian mode in a C major context) to add variety.

- **Inversions and Voicings**: Experimenting with chord inversions (changing the bass note) or voicings (the arrangement of notes) can add richness and depth to your progressions.

- **Cadences**: These are specific types of chord progressions that resolve musical phrases. For example, a perfect cadence (V-I) sounds final, while an imperfect cadence (I-V) feels open and unresolved.
