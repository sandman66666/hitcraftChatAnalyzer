   - **Shelving EQ**: Boosts or cuts frequencies above or below a certain point.
   - **High-pass/Low-pass Filters**: Cuts frequencies below (high-pass) or above (low-pass) a certain threshold.

2. **Common Frequency Ranges**:
   - **Sub-Bass (20-60 Hz)**: Adds weight, felt rather than heard.
   - **Bass (60-250 Hz)**: Fundamental for bass instruments and kick drums.
   - **Lower Midrange (250-500 Hz)**: Adds warmth, but can muddy mixes if overused.
   - **Midrange (500 Hz - 2 kHz)**: Critical for vocals and most instruments.
   - **Upper Midrange (2-4 kHz)**: Presence and clarity, especially for vocals.
   - **Treble (4-20 kHz)**: Airiness and brightness, but too much can lead to harshness.

### **Practical EQ Techniques**
- **Cut Before Boosting**: To avoid clutter, cut unwanted frequencies before boosting desired ones.
- **Use High-Pass Filters**: Apply high-pass filters to remove low-end rumble from non-bass instruments (e.g., guitars, vocals).
- **Notch Filtering**: If you hear a boxy sound, use a narrow Q to cut around 300-500 Hz for clarity.
- **Boost Sparingly**: When boosting, narrow the Q to maintain a more natural sound. 
- **Enhance Vocals**: Boost around 2-4 kHz for presence, but cut around 200-300 Hz if the vocals sound muddy.
