### Understanding Stereo Tracks:
1. **Stereo vs. Mono:** A stereo track contains two channels (left and right), allowing for a richer sound. If your vocals are on a stereo track, the panning is fixed across those two channels.

2. **Adjusting Stereo Tracks:**
   - **In a DAW:** If you have access to the original project file, you can either:
     - **Separate the Vocal Track:** If the vocals are recorded on a stereo track, you might need to split them into two mono tracks for independent control. Most DAWs allow you to do this.
     - **Use a Stereo Panning Tool:** If your DAW supports it, you can use stereo panning plugins to manipulate the stereo image more effectively.

3. **Effects on Stereo Tracks:**
   - Sometimes, effects like reverb or delay can create an illusion of space and may affect how the vocals sit in the mix. Ensure that these effects are also set up to complement your panning choices.

### Steps to Adjust Panning in Your DAW:
1. **Open the DAW:** Load the mixed track of your vocals.
2. **Locate the Vocal Track:** Identify the stereo track containing the vocals.
3. **Split the Track (if needed):** 
   - If your DAW allows it, split the stereo track into two mono tracks for individual control.
4. **Adjust Panning:** Use the panning controls for each track:
   - Move the left track slightly to the left and the right track slightly to the right to create space.
5. **Listen and Fine-Tune:** Play back the track and adjust until you find the perfect balance.

### Example of Vocal Panning:
For instance, if you want your lead vocals to be centered but add backing vocals slightly to the left and right, you could pan:
- Lead Vocals: Center (0%)
- Backing Vocals: Left (-15%) and Right (+15%)
